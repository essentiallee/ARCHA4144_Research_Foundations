Research Foundations: Art-based Gentrification

A Study on Artworld’s Soho Migration from 1950s to the 70s

Jae Hyun Lee | jl7559

MS.CDP 2026

Project overview

Research Description

This research examines SoHo’s transformation between the 1950s and late 1970s not as a simple narrative in which artists “caused” gentrification, but as a multi-stage conversion of industrial space into cultural value and cultural value into land value. It investigates how deindustrialization, threatened urban renewal, illegal loft occupation, artist organizing, zoning reform, media representation, gallery agglomeration, historic preservation, and real-estate policy interacted over time. The project combines archival and policy research with a geocoded dataset of artist residences, galleries, alternative spaces, rents, property transactions, loft conversions, and demographic change. Its main hypothesis is that artistic activity becomes a mechanism of gentrification only when inexpensive adaptable space, regulatory change, concentrated cultural networks, public recognition, and weak tenure protections occur together. Artists produced spatial improvements, social networks, and symbolic identity, but the resulting value was largely captured by property owners and commercial actors. The final section compares SoHo’s limited organizational victories with artist-governed institutions that established stronger control over housing, property, membership, or exhibition decisions.
